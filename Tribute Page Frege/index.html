Gottlob Frege
Pioneer of modern formal logic

Friedrich Ludwig Gottlob Frege (8 November 1848 – 26 July 1925) was a German philosopher and mathematician who worked at the University of Jena. He invented modern quantificational logic, created the first-ever axiomatic system for logic, and made contributions to the foundations of mathematics. He is also the originator of the analytic tradition in philosophy, where he concentrated on the philosophy of language and the philosophy of mathematics.
Though Frege didn't gain much prominence during his lifetime, Giuseppe Peano (1858–1932) and Bertrand Russell (1872–1970) introduced his work to later generations of logicians and philosophers, mainly through Russell's and Whitehead's epoch-making Principia Mathematica. The Frege programming language (a Haskell dialect for the Java virtual machine) is named after him.

Timetable

1848 Born in Wismar.
1869 Graduates from Gymnasium and enrolls at the University of Jena.
1873 Receives a Ph. D. in mathematics from the University of Göttingen under the supervision of Ernst Schering.
1874 Back in Jena, Frege completes his Habilitationsschrift, entitled Rechnungsmethoden, die sich auf eine Erweiterung des Größenbegriffes gründen (“Methods of Calculation Based on an Extension of the Concept of Quantity”) and becomes Privatdozent (lecturer) at the University of Jena.
1879 Publishes his first book, Begriffsschrift, eine der arithmetischen nachgebildete Formelsprache des reinen Denkens (“Concept Notation: A formula language of pure thought, modeled upon that of arithmetic”). The Begriffsschrift is the first in a series of publications that contribute to Frege's overarching goal of showing that arithmetic is deducible from logical axioms. It is the first formalized quantificational logic of history, couched in a peculiar two-dimensional notation Frege developed for the purpose. This unfamilar notation was probably one of the reasons why the Begriffsschrift didn't receive much attention from the broader public in its day.
Is promoted to außerordentlicher Professor (associate professor).
1884 Publishes his second book, Die Grundlagen der Arithmetik: eine logisch-mathematische Untersuchung über den Begriff der Zahl. In this book Frege provides a non-technical philosophical justification and outline of the ideas that he was about to develop technically in his two-volume work Grundgesetze der Arithmetik (1893/1903).
1892 Publishes the paper ‘On Sense and Reference’, dubbed “a primeval Text of modern semantics”.
1893 Publishes the first volume of his opus magnum, Grundgesetze der Arithmetik.

If you want to learn more about Frege, read the article about him in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, or read Logicomix: An Epic Search for Truth (Apostolos Doxiadis, Christos Papadimitriou et al.), a graphic novel about the foundational crisis in mathematics.
